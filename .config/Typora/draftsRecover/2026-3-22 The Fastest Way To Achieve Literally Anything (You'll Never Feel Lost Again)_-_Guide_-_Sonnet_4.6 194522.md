# The Fastest Way To Achieve Literally Anything

> You'll Never Feel Lost Again - The Goal-Directed Framework

Rian Doris argues that the human mind's default state is one of disorder and despair — what psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi called "psychic entropy." The antidote is becoming _goal-directed_: structuring your consciousness around a hierarchy of goals that cascade from an infinite, unattainable purpose all the way down to the actions you take in the next hour. Doris lays out a practical system called the **Goal Stack**, built through three steps: expanding your possibility space (what you believe is achievable), reverse-engineering goals from purpose down to daily tasks, and executing through _goal-directed actions_ — the single highest-leverage thing you can do at any moment to move toward your goals. The system culminates in a nightly **Power-Down Ritual** that plans the next day's actions before you stop working. Goal-directedness isn't just a productivity technique; it's presented as a prerequisite for flow state, psychological stability, and meaningful performance.

##  The Origin: Psychic Entropy and a Head Injury

Doris opens with a personal story. At 13, he had unusual clarity of purpose: training six days a week to become a professional rugby player, also surfing with religious dedication. His identity and attention were fully channeled through those pursuits. Then a head injury nearly killed him. For six years he was chronically debilitated — blurred vision, amnesia, unable to function — but the worst part, he says, was psychological: the loss of a future point to orient around.

By 15, with no goals and no identity left, his mind began to unravel. He describes having an overwhelming impulse one morning to jump off his train to school, walk into the fields, and just keep walking — a desperate attempt to escape the internal chaos. He later identified this as what Csikszentmihalyi termed **psychic entropy**: the natural tendency of human consciousness to drift into disorder, chaos, and despair when left unstructured. Doris emphasizes that Csikszentmihalyi regarded this not as a temporary condition but as the mind's _default setting_. Without goals to organize it, consciousness ruminated, worried, and spiraled.

The breakthrough came when Doris stumbled on a self-help book about goal-setting and, fairly arbitrarily, decided to become a doctor. As that goal took hold, the chaos receded. He slept better, ate better, became more selective about his time and relationships. Everything in his day began orienting toward that North Star — even though he never actually became a doctor.

## Goal-Directedness: The Core Concept

Being **goal-directed** means having a high percentage of your daily actions tied directly to your long-term vision. Doris frames it neurologically: goals activate the prefrontal cortex (decision-making and focus), suppress the brain's default mode network (the source of worry and rumination), and light up reward centers — creating a sustained sense of purpose. He also describes it as the prerequisite for flow: "The goals are the pipe through which your consciousness flows. Without goals, there's no flow."

The key insight from his personal story is that the _specific_ goal matters less than _having_ one. Waiting for certainty about whether a goal is the "right" one is one of the biggest blockers to becoming goal-directed. The purpose of a goal is to structure consciousness first; accuracy can be iterated on later.

## The Goal Stack

Rather than simple goal-setting, Doris proposes building a **Goal Stack** — a structured hierarchy that links an unattainable infinite purpose at the top to daily, hourly, and even minute-to-minute actions at the bottom. The layers are: **Purpose → High-Hard Goals → Annual → Quarterly → Monthly → Weekly → Daily Goal-Directed Actions**. Each step must genuinely support the one above it; a missing step means the staircase leads nowhere.

### Step 1: Expand Your Possibility Space

Before setting goals, you have to widen your internal map of what's achievable. Doris uses his brother Caelan as the central illustration. Three boys played rugby at the same skill level. One aimed for the county team, one for the provincial team, and eight-year-old Caelan wanted to captain Ireland in the World Cup. Because Caelan's _possibility space_ — the full range of futures he believed were accessible to him — was so much larger, he took actions the others would never have conceived of: seeking elite coaches, attending Blackrock College, uprooting his life. Caelan did eventually captain Ireland and play in the Rugby World Cup. The talent was roughly equal; the difference was in what each boy believed was within reach.

Doris cites Elon Musk's trajectory as a second example: without the belief that radically cheaper rockets were achievable, Musk would never have taken the sequence of actions that led from PayPal to Falcon 1 to reusable orbital rockets to Starship.

He offers three methods for expanding your possibility space:

**1. Belief Contagion (Social Contagion):** Beliefs are stored in the same neural regions involved in social processing. Surrounding yourself with people who have larger possibility spaces literally changes what your brain accepts as viable. The commonly cited social influence maxims ("you become the five people you spend the most time with") are, Doris argues, neurologically accurate.

**2. Brute Force Big Thinking:** Ask forcing questions: _What would 100x this goal look like? What would I attempt if I knew I couldn't fail? What goals have I dismissed as impossible?_ Philosopher Terence McKenna's question — _"What would I do if I could do anything?"_ — is cited as a useful prompt. Crucially, Doris argues you should set goals that exceed your _current_ resources, skills, and connections; those will change en route. Setting goals bounded by present constraints permanently anchors you to them.

**3. Goal Attainment and Self-Efficacy:** _Self-efficacy_ (your belief in your ability to achieve goals) compounds. Each goal you achieve expands what you believe is possible next. This is why having a Goal Stack matters even before you feel ready — you need goals in order to attain them, and you need to attain them in order to believe bigger.

### Step 2: Define Your Purpose (Pick a Problem, Not a Purpose)

Doris reframes purpose as a verb, not a noun — something you _do_, not something you _find_. Waiting to feel a calling is a trap that paralyzes people for years. His alternative: pick a problem you wish didn't exist in the world, ideally one you've personally encountered.

He uses the dinner menu analogy: some people arrive at a restaurant knowing exactly what they want; most don't. But you don't go hungry waiting for perfect certainty — you look at the menu and order something, and your tastes refine over time. He identifies examples of problem-to-purpose framing: plastic ocean pollution → _create a world where marine life thrives free from human impact_; mental health stigma → _ensure every person has resources for a healthy and dignified life_. Your current work can also constitute the problem you pick — an athlete advancing human performance limits, or an employee aligning with their company's stated mission.

The insight is that clarity about direction is a _choice_, not a discovery.

### Step 3: Set a High-Hard Goal and Reverse Engineer

Doris introduces the concept through the story of Michel Lotito — "Monsieur Mangetout" — who, beginning in 1978, spent two years eating an entire Cessna 150 airplane piece by piece. His method was reverse engineering: deconstruct the object into manageable sub-components, start with the most accessible piece, grind it down, take a spoonful. Doris uses this as a model for the Goal Stack process.

A **high-hard goal**, a term coined by sports psychologist Dr. Stanley Beecham (building on Edwin Locke's research that specific, challenging goals produce higher performance than vague or easy ones), is an ambitious objective requiring 1–5 years to achieve — something that forces real growth and demands full engagement. The Goal Stack is then built by reverse-engineering from that goal downward: the high-hard goal becomes annual targets, which become quarterly milestones, then monthly objectives, then weekly goals, then daily tasks. Each level must directly support the level above it.

Doris recommends treating this more like the **lean startup** approach than waterfall project planning: set a direction, sketch out the reverse-engineered steps as a best estimate, but hold plans loosely and refine as you go. The act of moving forward illuminates the right path; over-planning before execution is its own form of stagnation.

## Goal-Directed Actions: The Core Driver of Daily Performance

Having a Goal Stack structures consciousness at the macro level, but the daily execution problem remains: what do you actually work on right now? Doris introduces the concept of **goal-directed actions** — defined as the single action that will progress you more toward a given goal than any other conceivable action.

He illustrates with two entrepreneurs both targeting eight-figure businesses. Entrepreneur A drifts through her day — reacting to emails, following an impulse to improve branding after reading a message about it, exploring social media vendors without committing, bouncing without direction. Entrepreneur B, having decided the night before that her quarterly goal is lead generation, her monthly focus is Facebook ads, and her highest-leverage action is rewriting ad creative, sits down and does exactly that for three hours in flow, then ties up loose ends. Same industry, same goal — radically different trajectory.

Goal-directed actions also increase what Doris calls **perceived importance** — the personal significance assigned to a task. Higher perceived importance triggers increased dopamine, which sharpens attention and accelerates entry into flow state. This creates a self-reinforcing loop: more goal-directed actions → more flow → more productive output → faster goal attainment → expanded self-efficacy.

## Separating Planning from Doing

Doris argues that planning and doing require incompatible cognitive modes. Planning requires **divergent thinking** — creative, associative, generating options. Execution requires **convergent thinking** — narrowing attention onto a single thing. Mixing the two by deciding what to work on _while_ you're working inflates cognitive load, which actively blocks flow. He cites Daniel Kahneman's dual-process theory (System 1 for fast/intuitive doing, System 2 for deliberate/analytical planning) and John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory as the relevant scientific scaffolding.

The practical prescription is to plan the night before, not the morning of, and never during the work itself.

## The Power-Down Ritual

The system's operational center is a **10-minute end-of-day ritual** with four components:

1. **Avoid Goal Drift:** Review your Goal Stack and check whether today's activities were actually aligned with your high-hard goals. Course-correct any drift before it compounds.

2. **Line Up the Dominoes:** Identify the maximum three goal-directed actions for tomorrow — the tasks that move you farthest toward your goals. These should be challenging but achievable given your current capabilities.

3. **Set Clear Goals:** Break each goal-directed action into micro-goals — specific, sub-task-level steps that eliminate any decision-making at execution time. The example given: instead of "work on the presentation," the clear goals are "write the intro," "insert past client data," "design graphics with brand colors." Then take the _first step of the first step_ (e.g., open the document, title it, save it in the right folder) so that the next day you can begin immediately without warm-up friction.

4. **Clear Loose Ends:** Close out any undone tasks and clear the deck so tomorrow's work block starts clean.

Doris contrasts this with the average knowledge worker's approach: an arbitrary Monday to-do list determined by mood or a manager's requests, not anchored to any long-term vision, and rarely scheduled in a way that ensures execution.

## The Goal-Directed Pyramid

Doris synthesizes the entire framework into a three-tier pyramid:

- **Base — Goal Stack (Aspiration):** Your purpose and the full hierarchy of goals from infinite purpose down to weekly targets. This is the structural foundation — what you're ultimately building toward.
- **Middle — Goal-Directed Actions (Action):** The high-leverage tasks identified daily that directly propel you up the Goal Stack. The "glowing stepping stones" at the pyramid's core.
- **Summit — Clear Goals (Attention):** The wildly specific micro-steps that direct your attention moment to moment, trigger flow, and eliminate ambiguity.

The thesis that ties it together: getting into deep flow right now (the "deep now") requires clarity about the far future. And building the far future requires deep flow now. The two are mutually dependent, and the Goal Stack is the mechanism that binds them.

## Additional Context

_The following is external context not mentioned in the video, drawn from general knowledge._

**Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's foundational work** on flow and psychic entropy is drawn primarily from his 1990 book _Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience_. His concept of psychic entropy as the mind's default — and goal-directed attention as its remedy — is a central thesis of that book, not a peripheral observation. The book also argues that flow is among the most reliable paths to subjective well-being, which gives Doris's framework a deeper grounding than productivity optimization alone.

**Edwin Locke's Goal-Setting Theory**, developed with Gary Latham, is one of the most replicated findings in organizational psychology. Their 1990 book _A Theory of Goal Setting and Task Motivation_ formalized the conclusion that specific, difficult goals consistently outperform vague or easy ones — the empirical foundation for "high-hard goals."

**Daniel Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 framework** is developed in _Thinking, Fast and Slow_ (2011). The application here — that planning and doing should be temporally separated because they recruit different cognitive systems — is consistent with the research, though Kahneman himself focused more on judgment and decision-making than productivity design.

**The "lean startup" methodology** Doris references was formalized by Eric Ries in _The Lean Startup_ (2011), drawing on Toyota's production philosophy and Steve Blank's customer development model. Its core principle — build-measure-learn iteration over upfront comprehensive planning — maps well to Doris's advice to hold reverse-engineered sub-goals loosely.

**Caelan Doris** is a real person and an actual Irish rugby international, making the biographical anecdote verifiable rather than illustrative fiction. He has been a prominent figure in Irish rugby and played in major international tournaments.

**The Flow Research Collective** positions itself in a growing field of applied flow science. Other notable contributors include Steven Kotler (co-founder of the Flow Research Collective alongside Doris), whose books _The Rise of Superman_ and _Stealing Fire_ popularized flow science for general audiences. Kotler's and Csikszentmihalyi's work are the primary intellectual lineage for the organization's methods.

**Speaker:** Rian Doris, Co-Founder and CEO of the Flow Research Collective — an organization that trains individuals and organizations (including Audi, Accenture, and the US Air Force) to access flow states using neuroscience-based principles. _Flow state: a peak performance state of total absorption in a task, where focus and output are significantly amplified._
