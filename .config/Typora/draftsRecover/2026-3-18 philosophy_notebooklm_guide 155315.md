## Notebook 1: Pure Philosophy (Foundation)

This is the backbone. Sources from this notebook are duplicated into every analysis notebook as "base knowledge."

### Logic and Argumentation
- Patrick Hurley — *A Concise Introduction to Logic* (standard college textbook: deduction, induction, validity, soundness, fallacies, syllogisms, propositional/predicate logic)
- Graham Priest — *Logic: A Very Short Introduction* (Oxford; lighter alternative or supplement)
- Susan Haack — *Philosophy of Logics* (meta-level: what justifies logic itself, different logical systems)

### Epistemology (Theory of Knowledge)
- Robert Audi — *Epistemology: A Contemporary Introduction to the Theory of Knowledge* (rationalism, empiricism, foundationalism, coherentism, skepticism)
- Daniel Kahneman — *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (cognitive biases, heuristics — maps how real reasoning deviates from ideal reasoning)

### History of Philosophy (Textbooks)
- Anthony Kenny — *A New History of Western Philosophy* (4 volumes; thorough and modern)
- Bertrand Russell — *A History of Western Philosophy* (classic; readable but opinionated)
- Nigel Warburton — *Philosophy: The Basics* (accessible entry point)
- Nigel Warburton — *A Little History of Philosophy* (narrative-style overview)

### Primary Sources — Ancient
- Plato — *Republic*
- Aristotle — *Nicomachean Ethics*
- Marcus Aurelius — *Meditations*

### Primary Sources — Early Modern
- René Descartes — *Meditations on First Philosophy* (rationalist epistemology)
- David Hume — *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* (empiricist epistemology)
- Immanuel Kant — *Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics* (accessible synthesis of rationalism and empiricism; shorter than *Critique of Pure Reason*)
- Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Pure Reason* (optional; the full treatment)

### Primary Sources — Existentialist and Modern
- Friedrich Nietzsche — *Beyond Good and Evil*
- Friedrich Nietzsche — *On the Genealogy of Morals*
- Søren Kierkegaard — *Sickness Unto Death*
- Søren Kierkegaard — *Fear and Trembling*
- Jean-Paul Sartre — *Existentialism is a Humanism* (short, accessible lecture)
- Albert Camus — *The Myth of Sisyphus*
- Martin Heidegger — *Being and Time* (dense but foundational)

### Ethics and Political Philosophy
- John Stuart Mill — *Utilitarianism*
- Immanuel Kant — *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*
- John Rawls — *A Theory of Justice* (counterweight to individualist/libertarian sources)
- Karl Marx — *The Communist Manifesto* or selected writings (counterweight for balance)
- Emmanuel Levinas — *Totality and Infinity* or selected essays (ethics-first philosophy; counterweight to Nietzsche's individualism)

### Philosophy of Mathematics
- Stewart Shapiro — *Thinking About Mathematics* (discovered vs. invented, Platonism vs. formalism vs. empiricism)

### Philosophy of Science
- Peter Godfrey-Smith — *Theory and Reality* (what science is, how it works, its philosophical foundations)

### Reference Sources
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entries (free, peer-reviewed, thorough — add entries for any topic you want deeper coverage on)

## How to Use Each Notebook

### Pure Philosophy — Use For:
- Straight academic questions ("What is the categorical imperative?")
- Understanding schools of thought ("Explain the difference between deontology and consequentialism")
- Evaluating arguments structurally ("Is this argument valid?")
- Epistemological questions ("How did Hume challenge rationalism?")
- Logic practice ("What fallacy is this?")

### Individual Analysis Notebooks — Use For:
- Deep reading of one novel through a philosophical lens
- Connecting a novel's themes to formal philosophy
- Character analysis using philosophical frameworks
- Asking "what would [philosopher] say about [character/event]?"
- Tracing how a novel dramatizes or distorts a philosophical position

### Combined Notebook — Use For:
- Comparative questions across novels ("How does Roark's individualism differ from Raskolnikov's?")
- Tracing a theme across multiple thinkers and works ("How do Rand, Nietzsche, and Kierkegaard each handle the individual vs. society?")
- Finding unexpected connections between works
- Broad "survey course" style exploration

## Prompting Strategies

### Set the Epistemological Ground Rules Up Front
Use a pinned note or opening prompt in each notebook like:

> "When answering my questions, always evaluate arguments structurally — check for validity, soundness, hidden premises, and logical fallacies. If I make an assumption, challenge it. If a philosopher's argument has known weaknesses, state them. Do not tell me what I want to hear. Present opposing viewpoints when they exist. Distinguish between what a philosopher actually argued and popular misreadings of their work. When I ask for your analysis, show your reasoning and identify where the conclusion depends on debatable premises."

### For the Analysis Notebooks, Add:
> "When analyzing this novel, ground your interpretation in the philosophical source material provided. Do not speculate beyond what the sources support. If a character's actions align with a specific philosophical position, name it and cite the relevant philosopher. If there are multiple valid interpretations, present them and explain what each depends on."

### Question Frameworks That Get Strong Results

**For understanding a philosopher:**
- "Reconstruct [philosopher]'s argument for [claim] step by step. What are the premises? Is the argument valid? Sound? Where do critics attack it?"
- "What problem was [philosopher] trying to solve, and what existing solutions was he rejecting?"

**For analyzing fiction philosophically:**
- "What philosophical position does [character] embody? Where does the novel's portrayal align with and diverge from the formal philosophy?"
- "What is the strongest philosophical objection to [character]'s worldview using the sources in this notebook?"

**For cross-referencing (combined notebook):**
- "Compare how [Novel A] and [Novel B] dramatize the tension between [theme]. Which one is more philosophically rigorous and why?"
- "If [Philosopher A] read [Novel B], what would be their primary critique?"

**For logic and bias-checking:**
- "I believe [claim]. Using the epistemology and logic sources, evaluate this belief. What are the premises I'm assuming? Are any of them debatable? What's the strongest counterargument?"
- "What cognitive biases from Kahneman's work might be influencing how I'm reading [philosopher/novel]?"

**For synthesis:**
- "Based on all sources in this notebook, construct the strongest possible argument for [position]. Then construct the strongest possible argument against it."

## Where to Find Sources

- **Project Gutenberg** (gutenberg.org) — free public domain texts (most ancient and early modern philosophy, Dostoevsky, Nietzsche)
- **Internet Archive** (archive.org) — digital lending library for newer texts
- **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy** (plato.stanford.edu) — free, peer-reviewed, excellent supplementary entries
- **Local library digital lending** (Libby/OverDrive) — ebooks and audiobooks of most titles listed
- **Google Books** — preview/partial access to many academic works
- **PhilPapers** (philpapers.org) — academic philosophy paper database

Most of the pre-20th-century primary sources are freely available as PDFs. The textbooks, Cambridge Companions, and modern secondary works will require library access or purchase.

## Build Order Recommendation

1. **Start with Pure Philosophy** — get the foundation in place first
2. **Pick one analysis notebook** to build next (whichever novel or thinker you're most drawn to right now)
3. **Add more analysis notebooks** as you go — you don't need them all built on day one
4. **Build the combined notebook last** — it only becomes useful once you have multiple works loaded and have spent time with the individual notebooks first

This mirrors how a philosophy degree works: survey courses first, then seminars on individual thinkers, then advanced comparative work.
